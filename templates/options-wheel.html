How the Options Wheel Works

A systematic approach to generating income from stocks you want to own

Phase 1: Sell Cash-Secured Puts

Start by selling a put option at a strike price below the current price. You collect premium immediately and only need to buy shares if the stock falls below your strike. This is called cash-secured because you hold the full capital needed to buy the shares.

Key Benefit: You get paid to wait for the stock to reach your target buy price.

Phase 2: Get Assigned Shares

If the stock closes below your put strike at expiration, you are assigned 100 shares per contract at the strike price. This is actually good - you wanted the stock. Your true cost basis is reduced by every premium you've collected.

Key Benefit: Premium collected lowers your true purchase price below the strike.

Phase 3: Sell Covered Calls

Now that you own shares, sell covered calls at a strike above your cost basis. You collect more premium. If the stock stays below the call strike, you keep the shares and the premium. You keep selling calls every expiration cycle until shares are called away.

Key Benefit: Generate income from shares while you wait for price to recover.

Phase 4: Repeat the Cycle

If shares get called away, you've realized a full profit cycle. Cash resets and you start selling puts again. If shares aren't called away, keep selling calls each expiry - collecting premium repeatedly. Either way, you're earning.

Key Benefit: A repeating income machine with defined risk at every step.

Are Returns Guaranteed?

No. The wheel enforces disciplined buy-low, sell-high behavior. In falling markets you may accumulate shares below your cost basis temporarily. The real risk: a stock that drops dramatically and stays down for months while you hold 100+ shares. Only wheel stocks you'd own anyway - this strategy does not eliminate stock risk.

Key Risk: A prolonged drawdown while holding assigned shares can create large unrealized losses.

What Makes a Good Wheel Stock?

Stock You'd Own Long-Term

Rule #1. Only wheel stocks you'd genuinely be happy holding for months if assigned. Never wheel a stock just for its premium.

No Earnings Before Expiry

Earnings cause enormous overnight price swings. Check the earnings calendar before every trade. Never let an options contract expire through an unplanned earnings date.

Price $20–$150

Too cheap = tiny premiums in dollar terms. Too expensive = too much capital in one position. The sweet spot gives meaningful income without overconcentration.

Market Cap > $2B

Large-caps have tighter bid/ask spreads on options. You get better fill prices and can exit positions more easily.

IV Between 25–60%

Too low (<20%) and premiums don't justify the capital. Too high (>60%) and the market is pricing in a good reason for the volatility.

Sideways-to-Bullish Trend

The wheel performs best in flat-to-slightly-rising markets. A strong downtrend can keep you assigned at ever-lower prices, compounding losses.
